INTRODUCTION

RISE is a mentorship program that aims to facilitate the growth of junior scholars in low- and middle-income countries by connecting them to scholars from highly ranked universities in high-income countries as mentors.

Scholars at early stages often lack opportunities to seek feedback on their research, especially research guidance from established senior scientists. Nowhere is this more evident than for junior scholars in low- and middle-income countries. The situation has worsened during the COVID-19 pandemic, as opportunities to participate in and network at conferences and seminars have been sharply limited.

Specifically, RISE helps to establish first-step mentor-mentee discussions on a student’s working paper or manuscript prior to submission to journals. Junior scholars interested in this program are invited to register and upload their manuscripts. The program will match submissions to suitable potential mentors when available.

If you would like to benefit from this program, improve your scholarship, and make new connections, join us!

More detail about our process can be found in THE PROCESS and FAQ.

THE PROCESS

  • For Mentees
  • For Mentors

1. Applications are submitted by junior scholars interested in obtaining feedback from established scholars in their fields. Specifically, applicants should submit one working paper for review by a potential mentor.

2. Applications are reviewed and participants are notified whether a mentor has been found.

3. Mentor/mentee pairings are scheduled for a 30-min discussion about potential revisions and future research directions.

1. Mentors will receive a draft from a mentee who submitted a working paper matching the mentor’s expertise.

2. After critically reviewing the draft, mentors will be scheduled for a 30-minute discussion with the author to share their feedback. In order to make the review process efficient, sending written comments in advance is encouraged as well. 

Frequent Asked Questions

  • For  Mentees
  • For Mentors

1. Who are the targeted mentees?
Targeted mentees include Ph.D. students, postdocs, and junior faculty members affiliated with academic institutions in low- and middle-income countries who are looking for an opportunity to receive mentorship advice on specific papers.

2. Is there a cost for the mentorship program?
It is totally free.

3. Can I choose my mentor myself?
Currently, we do not have the resources to accommodate such requests. We will do our best to choose a suitable mentor for you in terms of research interest and if, for some reason, the connection is not working well you can request a re-match.

4. What will I do as a mentee?
Apply by clicking the sign-up link. Then, submit your draft as well as your personal information via the EasyChair system. We'll notify you within three weeks regarding next steps if we are able to find a match.

5. What benefits will I gain from this mentorship program?
1. Access to a structured mentorship relationship with scholars from highly-ranked institutions.
2. Constructive criticism on your publication drafts.
3. Trusted advice from your mentor on potentially promising areas for future scholarship.

1. Who are the mentors?
Our mentors are scholars from highly-ranked institutions.

2. Is there a cost for the mentorship program?
It is totally free.

3. Can I choose my mentee myself?
Currently, we do not have the resources to accommodate such requests. We will do our best to choose a suitable mentee for you in terms of research interest and if, for some reason, the connection is not working well, you can request a re-match.

4. What will I do as a mentor?
You will critically review a draft assigned to you within 21 days (3 weeks). Once finished reviewing this paper, you’ll be scheduled for a 30-minute discussion with the author to share your feedback. In order to make the review process efficient, sending written comments in advance is encouraged.

5. What benefits will I gain from this mentorship program?
1. Expand the thought diversity of science through mentorship.
2. Nurture the next generation of young scientists within your field.
3. Learn about the topics that junior scholars find most pressing, and meet potential future collaborators.
4. “The rose's in her hand, the flavor in mine.” In other words, passing on wisdom is among the most rewarding elements of the scientific process.
